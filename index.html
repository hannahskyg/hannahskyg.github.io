San Diego Data Scientist Finds Stories Others Miss
Hannah Sky Gonzalez brings a UCSD-trained eye for statistics and a designer's instinct for storytelling to every dataset she touches.
By Hannah Sky Gonzalez · San Diego, CA

In a discipline crowded with dashboards and bullet points, Hannah Sky Gonzalez has carved a different path — one where the model is never the end of the story, only the beginning. A data scientist based in San Diego, she trained at the University of California, San Diego, where she developed a rigorous foundation in statistics, machine learning, and the kind of relentless curiosity that no curriculum can fully teach.

Her toolkit spans Python, R, SQL, and a growing suite of ML frameworks, but what colleagues notice first is her ability to translate complexity into clarity. Whether she is building a churn prediction model, an NLP pipeline, or an exploratory analysis from scratch, the output is always designed with its audience in mind.

The best analysis does not just answer the question — it changes the question being asked.

Currently open to new opportunities, Hannah is looking for teams that care as much about communicating findings as they do about finding them — places where data science is a craft, not just a pipeline.

About This Site
A data scientist reflecting on her own portfolio — the most recursive form of analysis.
Why a Newspaper?

The newspaper is one of the oldest information architectures ever designed. Its hierarchy of headlines, decks, and body copy is engineered to let readers scan quickly, decide what matters, and then go deep.

That is exactly what a portfolio should do. Instead of yet another card-grid resume site, this format forces discipline: every story must justify its column inches. If it does not deserve space, it does not get it.

The broadsheet also has a certain honesty about it. It is not pretending to be an app. It is a document — the same way a piece of analysis is a document. Something to be read, considered, and set down.

Design Decisions
Black & White Serif Type No Frameworks Dark Mode Column Rules Fast Load Responsive

Every design choice serves a function. The black-and-white palette focuses attention on content rather than chrome. The serif typefaces carry editorial authority the subject demands.

The constrained palette means every use of red carries weight — a label here, a heading there. Like a correction or a breaking story, it interrupts deliberately.

A portfolio is an argument. Every design choice either supports or undermines that argument.
